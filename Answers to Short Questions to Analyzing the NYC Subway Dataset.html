Section 1. Statistical Test

1.1 Which statistical test did you use to analyze the NYC subway data? Did you use a one-tail or a two-tail P value? What is the null hypothesis? What is your p-critical value?

ANSWER: Mann-Whitney U-Test. The scipy.stats method used in Problem 3 Set 3 returns a one-tailed P value [2]. The null hypothesis is that the ridership distribution on rainy days is equal to the ridership distribution on non-rainy days. The pcritical value was set to 0.05.

1.2 Why is this statistical test applicable to the dataset? In particular, consider the assumptions that the test is making about the distribution of ridership in the two samples.

ANSWER: With the Mann-Whitney U-Test there is no assumption that the samples have normal distribution. Since this is precisely the case (ridership distributions on rainy, or non-rainy days, cannot be considered normally distributed) this was the statistical analysis of choice.

1.3 What results did you get from this statistical test? These should include the following numerical values: p-values, as well as the means for each of the two samples under test.

ANSWER (Revised): Means of hourly entries when raining and when not raining were 1105.45 and 1090.28 respectively. U = 1924409167. With pcritical set to 0.05 two tailed; the returned one tailed value p = 0.0249999, multiplied by 2 equals 0.0499998, in order to compare it to pcritical. This value is less than pcritical; therefore rejecting null hypothesis of equal distribution.

1.4 What is the significance and interpretation of these results?

ANSWER (Revised): With a 95% confidence, the null hypothesis of samples having the same distribution is rejected; thus, accepting the alternative hypothesis that ridership on rainy days is statistically different from ridership on non-rainy days. However, the proximity of the two tailed test value of p to pcritical makes this a pretty close call.

Section 4. Conclusion

Please address the following questions in detail. Your answers should be 1-2 paragraphs long.

4.1 From your analysis and interpretation of the data, do more people ride
the NYC subway when it is raining or when it is not raining?  

ANSWER: Following the observation that the distributions for hourly entries on rainy and non-rainy days can not be considered normal, the non-parametric statistical test Mann-Whitney U-Test was performed, testing the null hypothesis that the sample of ridership on rainy days has the same distribution as the sample of ridership on non-rainy days. The alternative hypothesis is that one of these distributions is fundamentally larger than the other.

The result in the test leads to the affirmation that with 95% of confidence, the null hypothesis is rejected. That is to say, that with 95% of confidence, ridership is different on rainy and non-rainy days. Verifying the means of the samples, there is on average 15.17 more entries on rainy days.

4.2 What analyses lead you to this conclusion? You should use results from both your statistical

tests and your linear regression to support your analysis.

ANSWER: The result obtained by the statistical test is corroborated by the results obtained in the OLS linear regression; the variable corresponding to 'rain' is significant (p=0.045 < 0.05); also, the coefficient is positively correlated, meaning that ridership is on average larger on rainy days.

The coefficient in the linear regression of 64.63 means that, on average, ridership increases in 65 when it is raining, a greater quantity than the difference of sample means.


Section 5. Reflection

Please address the following questions in detail. Your answers should be 1-2 paragraphs long.

5.1 Please discuss potential shortcomings of the methods of your analysis, including:

  1. Dataset,
  2. Analysis, such as the linear regression model or statistical test.

ANSWER: The dataset is relatively large in terms of the number of turnstiles it covers; however, it can be argued that in the time dimension it falls short as it only covers one month. Throughout the year the weather variables may vary significantly more than in just one month, and an even better dataset would cover many years.

A non-parametric statistical test, such as the one that is used in this analysis, is less powerful than a parametric test; that is to say that such tests are less likely to find a difference [10]. This may explain why the null hypothesis rejection was a close call.

(Revised) The linear regression models assume a linear fit; yet this model may very well be more complex. As seen in question 2.6, the long tail in the histogram of residuals shows that there are quite a few very large differences between actual and predicted values. Aditionally, a plot of residuals per data point [13], shows a divergent pattern of increasing residuals.

A polynomial model that takes into account non-linearities may be a better prediction model, however in this case one should be aware of overfitting; that is when the model fits too well the dataset but has poor predictive power. In OLS, putting too many features can erode its performance [11], this is also why the model in this analysis has only two features, along with the fact that several weather variables may not be entirely independent, thus potentially creating multicollinearity.
